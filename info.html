Intro

        The Vikings, also known as Northmen and Varangians (especially by the Greeks/East Slavs), were Nordic seafarers from the European region of Scandinavia who raided and traded throughout most of northern, central, and eastern Europe during the late 8th to late 11th centuries, a period often known as the Viking Age. The term also has been extended to include the inhabitants of Viking home communities in Scandinavia. The military, mercantile, and demographic expansion of the Viking Age had a significant effect on the development of much of Europe.

        Seafaring Vikings were made up of land-owning chieftains, clan heads, their retainers, freemen, and anyone who sought adventure and riches overseas. While at home, most Vikings were independent, loosely organized independent farmers, at sea they were highly organized bands and armies of raiders and pillagers driven by leaders of ability. They navigated the coast of Europe, mounting raids on coastline cities, which earned them the name vikingr, meaning “pirate” in Old Norse.

        Viking activities, due to the aid of the longship, extended into the Mediterranean, North Africa, the Middle East, and Central Asia. This exploration facilitated the development of settlements stretching from European Russia to as far east as North America, where they frequently assimilated into the culture. This period of expansion allowed for the spread of Viking culture, and the introduction of foreign cultures into Scandinavian culture, as well as the Christianization of the pagan Vikings.

        Popular conception of the Vikings is much different than what is presented by archaeology and historical sources. Contemporary representations often paint the Vikings as “noble savages”, and they are alternately portrayed as violent heathens and great adventurers. Current popular representations rely on clichés and stereotypes, greatly confusing the picture of the Vikings.

History

        The Viking Age is usually defined from the early 790s to 1066, when the Normans, Viking descendants who controlled Normandy in northern France, invaded England. The period was defined by Viking exploration of Europe via seas and rivers for purposes of trading, raiding, colonizing, and conquering. The expansion may have been driven by retaliation against Christian missionaries for encroaching on tribal lands, or by overpopulation, changes in trade patterns and profitability, and the lack of viable farmland in their homeland. Information about the Viking Age is drawn mostly from archaeology, what was written about them by their enemies, and secondary sources like the Icelandic Sagas, prose narratives mostly recorded in the 14th and 15th centuries.

        The first recorded Viking raid was in 789, when a group of Norwegian men landed on the Isle of Portland, and, upon being mistaken as merchants, murdered a royal official. The beginning of the Viking Age in England, however, is dated to June 9, 793 when Vikings destroyed the abbey on Lindisfarne, a center of learning on an island off of England’s northeast coast. The monks were killed, thrown in the sea, or taken away as slaves along with the abbey’s riches. Northumbrian scholar Alcuin of York wrote of the raid, “Never before in Britain has such a terror appeared.” From that point on, the Vikings were regarded as “wolves among sheep”, and continued to raid abbeys and churches in the British Isles.

The Scandinavian territorial expansion during the Viking Age.

        In the western seas, the Viking expansion touched almost every possible point, from Iceland, to Greenland, to settlements as far west as North America. Settlements also sprung up in the Orkney, Faroe, and Shetland islands, the Hebrides, and the Isle of Man.

        France, under Charlemagne and his descendants, was also troubled by the Vikings. At the end of Charlemagne’s reign, the Vikings commenced a series of raids culminating in the conquest and settlement of Normandy. In 911, the French king Charles the Simple made an agreement with the Viking chief Rollo that gave him and his followers possession of Normandy in exchange for his fealty, conversion to Christianity, and their defense against other Viking groups. The Normans would later conquer both England, in 1066, and Ireland, in 1169.

        The Vikings traveled along the river-valleys of Northern Europe, settling, trading, and raiding as they went. They also expanded east, eventually founding the East Slavic state of Kievan Rus’. Other Vikings continued south to the Black Sea to Constantinople, trading extensively with the Byzantines.

        The trading, raiding, and settling of the Vikings touched mostly Scandinavia, England, the Slavic states, and the western coast of Europe, founding well established settlements in Iceland, Greenland, Great Britain, and Normandy. In addition, the eastern expansions of the Vikings allowed for the creation of Kievan Rus’ and the assimilation of the Vikings into Slavic culture. Central Europe, while mostly untouched by the Vikings due to powerful nations there. However, almost none of Europe was left unaffected by the Viking Age. From the Varangian Guard of the Byzantines to the settlement of England, Europe was forever changed by Viking influence.

Eventual Scandinavian settlement.

        Over the three centuries of the Viking Age, Scandinavian culture underwent drastic changes. Christianity has taken hold in the previously pagan region, and the Catholic Church had affirmed the establishment of monarchies in Norway, Sweden, and Denmark. The cause of Christianity spread quickly through Scandinavia, with the establishment of ecclesiastical administrative centers, dioceses, and archbishoprics. Old ideas and ideologies were changing.

        Christianity altered the aspirations of Scandinavian rulers, and how they viewed their neighbors. Much of the Viking raids had been focused on enslaving those who they conquered, but the Christian ban on chattel slavery disincentivized such activity, and raiding fell markedly. The Scandinavian peoples of Norway, Sweden, and Denmark would no longer be called Vikings.
